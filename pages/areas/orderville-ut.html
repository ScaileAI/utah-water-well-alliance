Water Well Drilling & Pump Services in Orderville, Utah

Utah Water Well Alliance serves Orderville and the Long Valley corridor of Kane County with expert water well drilling, pump installation, and around-the-clock emergency response. Nestled between the Grand Staircase–Escalante National Monument and the East Fork of the Virgin River, Orderville's rural properties depend heavily on private wells where municipal water infrastructure is limited or absent.

Orderville, Kane County

Long Valley Well Experts

Orderville sits in the heart of Long Valley, a broad north-south corridor carved by the East Fork of the Virgin River through layers of Jurassic and Cretaceous limestone, sandstone, and mudstone. This sedimentary geology produces wells that typically range from 400 to 700 feet deep — deeper than many other Southern Utah communities — as drillers often must penetrate multiple rock layers to reach productive fractured zones. Understanding local stratigraphy is critical to selecting the right drilling location and casing program.

With the Grand Staircase–Escalante National Monument encompassing much of the surrounding landscape, groundwater recharge patterns here are tied closely to seasonal precipitation and snowmelt from the high plateaus. Properties along the valley floor may tap alluvial sediments from the river corridor, while upland parcels typically require deeper drilling into fractured limestone or sandstone. Limited municipal water service in much of Long Valley means private wells are the backbone of domestic water supply for Orderville residents and neighboring communities.

Local Knowledge

What Drillers Need to Know About Orderville

  • ▶Layered sedimentary geology requires drilling expertise. Long Valley's geology consists of alternating limestone, sandstone, and mudstone formations deposited over millions of years. Productive aquifer zones typically occur in fractured limestone or porous sandstone layers, and locating these zones requires experience with the regional stratigraphy. Drilling blindly in this terrain risks dry holes or inadequate yield wells.
  • ▶Municipal water is limited — private wells are the primary supply. Much of Long Valley lacks the piped municipal infrastructure common in larger Utah cities. Orderville and neighboring communities like Glendale and Mt. Carmel Junction rely heavily on private groundwater. This makes proper well construction, casing integrity, and sanitary seals especially important for protecting the area's groundwater quality.
  • ▶Grand Staircase proximity shapes recharge patterns. The Grand Staircase–Escalante National Monument's high plateaus serve as recharge zones for the aquifer systems underlying Long Valley. Seasonal snowmelt and monsoon infiltration through fractured rock feeds the deep wells that serve Orderville. Understanding recharge timing helps explain seasonal variation in well yields across the area.
